Now the old people, the ones who lived where the winter could kill you, they could not afford to waste two thirds of anything. So they asked a simple question, the question that changes everything. What if instead of letting all that heat rush up the chimney, we made it stay? What if we forced that hot smoke to give up its heat to us before we ever let it leave the house? They built the stove out of mass, out of weight, out of brick and stone and clay and tile, tons of it. They built a long winding path, a maze of channels turning back and forth, up and down, twisting through the heart of all that masonry, so the hot smoke from the fire had to travel ten, fifteen, twenty feet of twisting passage through solid brick before it could ever find the chimney and escape. And as that hot smoke crawled and curled its way through all that stone, it did exactly what we wanted. It gave up its heat, it pressed its warmth into the brick at every turn, all along that long path, until by the time the smoke finally reached the chimney, it was so cooled, so spent, you could near hold your hand over it. The heat that a metal stove throws away, that whole two-thirds, this stove caught it, it trapped it in the brick, it kept it, and that is when the magic happens. Because now you have a mountain of brick, tons of it, soaked full of heat like a stone, soaks up the sun on a summer day, and brick does not give up its heat fast the way thin metal does. Brick holds it, brick lets it go slow, so slow, so gently, so evenly, radiating out into the rooms for hour after hour after hour. You burn your fire hot and quick in the morning, an hour or two, no more, and you let it go out. The fire is gone by breakfast, but the heat the fire made is not gone. It is locked up in that great mass of warm brick, and it will pour out of that brick, soft and steady, all the day long and all through the night, until you light your little fire again the next morning. That is how one fire heats a house for a whole day. The fire doesn't heat your house, the fire heats the brick, and the brick heats your house long after the fire is dead.
